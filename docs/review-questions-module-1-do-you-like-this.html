Review Questions: Module 1 (Do you like this?)

Write your answers in the boxes below. Tick the checkbox if you believe your answer is correct. Click Show model answers to compare.

(i) What is a variable? What is a value of a variable? Give examples.
(ii) How can we tell if a variable is quantitative, categorical, ordinal, or an identifier?
(iii) What is the difference between a statistic and a parameter?
(iv) What is the difference between a representative sample and a convenience sample?
(v) What is a contingency (two‑way) table? When is it used?
(vi) What is meant by marginal, joint, and conditional distributions?
Model answers

(i) A variable is any characteristic of a case (an individual or object being described). A value of a variable is the observed measurement or category for that case. For example, for the variable height, values are measurements in centimetres or metres. For country of origin, values may be Australia or Not Australia.

(ii) Quantitative variables takes on numerical values for which mathematical operations (like +, ÷) make sense, has a unit of measurement Categorical variables are defined by categories. Ordinal variables are categorical with a natural/set order. Identifiers uniquely label each case.

(iii) A statistic is a numerical measure calculated from a sample. A parameter is a numerical measure describing a population. Statistics are calculated by taking a sample of the population and collecting data. Parameters rely on data collected in a census.

(iv) A representative sample is representative of the target population. It can best be obtained by taking a random sample, where every individual in the population has an equally likely chance of being chosen to be in the sample. Advantage = unbiased sample; disadvantage = may be a complicated procedure to get the sample. A convenience sample includes individuals who are convenient for the researcher to sample. Because every individual in the population does not have an equally likely chance of being chosen, the sample is unlikely to be representative of the population and bias can occur. Advantage = convenient; disadvantage = biased sample.

(v) A contingency (two-way) table is a visual representation of data which shows the distribution of individuals along two categorical variables.

(vi) A marginal distribution describes proportion of individuals in the table located in one particular row or column. A joint distribution describes the proportion of individuals in the table located in one particular cell. A conditional distribution describes the proportion of individuals in a row/column located in one particular cell.
