1.1 Review Questions Module 1

Once you have watched the lecture recordings you should be able to answer the following Threshold questions:

  1. What is a variable? What is a value of a variable? Give examples of each.

  2. How can we tell if a variable is quantitative, categorical, ordinal or an identifier?

  3. What is the difference between a statistic and a parameter? What do they measure and how might they be obtained?

  4. What is the difference between a representative sample and a convenience sample? How are they obtained and what are their relative advantages/disadvantages?

  5. What is a contingency or two-way table? When is it used?

  6. What is meant by a marginal distribution, a joint distribution and a conditional distribution?

Note that stratified sampling involves taking simple random samples from each of a number of groups (strata) into which a population has been divided.

This question can be done manually, or can be done visually using the App (link below).

Suppose a random sample of eight people is required.

  1. Using a random number generator (or the App), select a simple random sample (SRS) from the sampling frame defined above.

  2. How many males and females are there in your sample?

  3. Using a random number generator (or the App), select a second simple random sample.

  4. How many males and females are there in this sample?

  5. Compare your two samples: Did you expect the result you got? Explain your answer with reference to the sampling process.

  6. *Using a random number generator (or the App), select a stratified random sample, stratified according to gender so as to contain equal numbers of males (m) and females (f).

  7. *Describe, in detail, the steps you took to produce your stratified sample.

  8. *Why might you decide that a stratified sample is more representative?

1.2.3 Question 2: Investigating the association between two categorical variables

Is there an association between the amount of coffee consumed and the number of hours of sleep students manage to have per night?

The following data was collected and organized into the Contingency Table (two-way table) below to see if there is an association between amount of coffee consumed (stated as ‘3 or less coffees per day (Light)’ and ‘more than 3 coffees per day (Heavy)’) and the number of hours slept per night (stated as ‘7 or less hours per night’ and ‘more than 7 hours per night’) for 151 statistics students.

Coffee consumption and hours of sleep
7 hours or less per night More than 7 hours per night Total
3 or less coffees per day (Light) 57 19 76
More than 3 coffees per day (Heavy) 36 39 75
Total 93 58 151
  1. From the data in the study,
  1. how many students slept 7 or less hours per night?
  2. how many students are light coffee drinkers?
  3. how many students are heavy coffee drinkers AND sleep 7 or less hours per night?
  1. From the data in the study (for each state if it is a joint, marginal or conditional percentage as well as the answer),
  1. what percentage of students slept 7 or less hours per night?
  2. what percentage of students are light coffee drinkers?
  3. what percentage of students are heavy coffee drinkers AND sleep 7 or less hours per night?
  4. what percentage of students who are light coffee drinkers also sleep 7 or less hours per night?
  5. what percentage of students who sleep 7 or less hours per night are light coffee drinkers?
